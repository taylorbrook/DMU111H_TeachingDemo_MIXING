## 2. DAW USAGE ACROSS UNIVERSITY PROGRAMS

### Summary Table

| DAW | Dominance Area | Universities Using It |
|-----|---------------|----------------------|
| **Pro Tools** | Professional studio recording, engineering, post-production | Berklee, MTSU, Elon, Full Sail, Musicians Institute, Drexel, McGill (Sound Recording), LIPA, Abbey Road Institute |
| **Logic Pro** | Composition, production (Mac-equipped schools) | Berklee, Elon, Full Sail, NYU, UCLA Extension, ICMP London, LIPA |
| **Ableton Live** | Electronic music production, live performance | Berklee, Drexel, MTSU, Foothill College, UCLA Extension, Liberty University, LIPA, Ethan Hein courses (NYU/Montclair) |
| **Reaper** | Budget-friendly, concept-transferable education | NYU Steinhardt, U. of San Diego, Berklee Online (accepted), growing in education |
| **Max/MSP** | Computer music, algorithmic composition, interactive media | UBC, Berklee (advanced), many graduate programs |
| **GarageBand** | Introductory courses, K-12 pipeline | U. of Utah MUSC 1340, many intro courses |
| **Soundtrap** | K-12, accessible/browser-based contexts | Spotify-marketed for education |
| **FL Studio** | Accepted at many programs | Berklee Online (accepted), community colleges |
| **Cubase** | Accepted at many programs | Berklee Online (accepted) |

### Key Patterns

- **Pro Tools** dominates programs focused on professional studio work and leads with 1,500+ Avid Learning Partners worldwide
- **Logic Pro** is primary at Mac-equipped schools and composition/production-oriented programs
- **Ableton Live** is the standard for electronic music production and performance courses
- **Reaper** is gaining ground as the cost-effective, platform-agnostic option ($60 educational license)
- **Many programs now teach multiple DAWs** or allow student choice, focusing on transferable concepts rather than platform-specific skills

## 4. CROSS-REFERENCE: WHICH BOOKS APPEAR IN OUR RESEARCH DOCUMENTS

All six books analyzed in our "Book Analysis - Commonalities and Contrasts" document are used in university programs. Full text PDFs for all six are available in the project's `resources/` folder.

| Book | Author | Edition in Resources | University Appearances |
|------|--------|---------------------|----------------------|
| **Mixing Audio: Concepts, Practices, and Tools** | Roey Izhaki | 4th (2024) | MIT 21M.380 (required reading), Wichita State LibGuides, endorsed by faculty at U. of Huddersfield and U. of Castilla-La Mancha |
| **Understanding and Crafting the Mix: The Art of Recording** | William Moylan | 1st (2002) | Author is Prof. at UMass Lowell (SRT program). Routledge companion website. Used in recording programs but specific syllabus appearances less documented online. |
| **Mixing with Your Mind** | Michael Paul Stavrou | 1st (2003) | Self-published; not found in formal syllabi but widely referenced in professional development contexts and recommended reading lists |
| **The Mixing Engineer's Handbook** | Bobby Owsinski | 5th (2022) | "Standard textbook in college recording and media programs around the world." Instructor's Resource Kit (syllabus, PowerPoints, quizzes for 12-week semester). |
| **Mixing Secrets for the Small Studio** | Mike Senior | 2nd (2019) | MIT 21M.380 required reading. cambridge-mt.com companion with 500+ multitracks. Explicitly "designed to be perfect for school and college use." |
| **Modern Recording Techniques** | David Miles Huber & Robert E. Runstein | 7th (2010) | Found "in almost every AE/music production class." 101 reference textbook for 30-40 years. Now in 10th edition (2023). |

### Books from our research documents ranked by university adoption:

1. **Huber & Runstein's Modern Recording Techniques** -- the most broadly adopted single textbook across all audio engineering programs (30-40 years)
2. **Bobby Owsinski's Mixing Engineer's Handbook** -- has the most established instructor support (official syllabus, PowerPoints, quizzes for 12-week semester) and is described as "the go-to text" for two decades
3. **Mike Senior's Mixing Secrets / Recording Secrets** -- appears in MIT syllabus AND has the strongest educational support infrastructure (cambridge-mt.com with 500+ multitracks, explicit "designed for school and college use")
4. **Izhaki's Mixing Audio** -- appears in the most formal syllabi (MIT, WSU, multiple endorsements)
5. **Moylan's Understanding and Crafting the Mix** -- used in academic recording programs, especially at the graduate level
6. **Stavrou's Mixing with Your Mind** -- professional development / recommended reading rather than formal adoption

## 6. KEY FINDINGS & PATTERNS

### Textbook Adoption Patterns

1. **No single textbook dominates** all music technology education. The field is too broad -- recording, mixing, electronic production, computer music, and sound design each have their own canonical texts.

2. **For recording/engineering courses**: Huber & Runstein's *Modern Recording Techniques* is the clear standard (30-40 years of adoption).

3. **For mixing-focused courses**: Three books share the space -- Owsinski's *Mixing Engineer's Handbook* (most widespread, best instructor support), Senior's *Mixing Secrets* (best practical support via cambridge-mt.com), and Izhaki's *Mixing Audio* (most comprehensive, strong in academic settings).

4. **For general intro courses**: Hosken's *An Introduction to Music Technology* and Williams & Webster's *Experiencing Music Technology* are the primary textbook options, though many instructors (e.g., Ethan Hein) use no textbook at all, relying on online readings.

5. **Trend toward no required textbook**: Several programs use online-only readings, free resources, and project-based learning rather than a single assigned text.

### DAW Adoption Patterns

1. **Pro Tools remains the industry-standard teaching DAW** for recording and engineering programs, reinforced by Avid's 1,500+ Learning Partner network.

2. **Logic Pro** is the primary DAW for production-oriented programs at Mac-equipped institutions.

3. **Ableton Live** is the clear standard for electronic music production and performance courses.

4. **Reaper is the emerging budget option** -- platform-agnostic, $60 educational license, growing adoption at institutions looking to reduce costs.

5. **Multi-DAW or DAW-agnostic teaching is increasing** -- many programs now let students choose their DAW or explicitly teach concepts that transfer across platforms.

### Geographic Patterns

- **North American programs** tend to be more vocational/industry-focused, with specific DAW training and professional certification paths (Avid, Apple)
- **UK programs** (especially Tonmeister at Surrey, Leeds, LIPA) integrate stronger science/engineering components alongside practical skills
- **Canadian programs** (McGill, UBC) lean more toward computer music, DSP, and research alongside practical recording
- **French-language programs** (UdeM) emphasize electroacoustic composition and media arts, with strong connections to the creative industries

### What the Research Documents Cover That Syllabi Confirm

Our three existing research documents align well with what universities actually teach:

- **Book Analysis**: The Moylan/Izhaki/Stavrou triangle maps directly to how universities structure mixing education -- analytical framework (Moylan/academic courses), technical competence (Izhaki/applied courses), and creative intuition (Stavrou/studio apprenticeship)
- **Mixing Skills Resources**: SoundGym, Cambridge-MT multitracks, EQ trainers, and iZotope tools appear repeatedly as supplementary resources in university programs
- **Open Access Resources**: MIT OCW, Coursera/Berklee MOOCs, and free multitrack libraries are used as primary or supplementary materials across programs at all levels
